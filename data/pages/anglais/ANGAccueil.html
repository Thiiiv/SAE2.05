Cordoba

Cordoba is a city located in Andalusia (southern region of Spain), and the capital of the province of the same name. It is one of the most populated cities in Andalusia with 326 039 inhabitants in 2020. The city is known for its rich architectural and cultural heritage that evoke the different phases of its history. Indeed, the city was built by the Romans and then ruled by the Muslims.

Cordoba was founded in the 2nd century BC by the Romans, the city acquired great importance in the time of Augustus. In the 8th century, it became the capital of the emirate dependent on Damascus founded by the Umayyad prince Abd al-Rahman I Then in 929, Abd al-Rahman III established the city as the seat of the caliphate. During this period (which is described as the glorious period of Cordoba) the construction of 300 mosques and countless palaces and public buildings began. The city rivals the splendors of Constantinople, Damascus and Baghdad. In the 13th century, under Ferdinand III, the Great Mosque of Córdoba was built, in addition to the Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos or the Calahorra Tower. The city is known for its heritage combining many artistic values from the East and West and including elements previously unknown in Islamic religious architecture. The historic center is one of the largest historic centers in Europe. In 1984, UNESCO declared the Cathedral Mosque a World Heritage Site. Later, in 1994, UNESCO extended this designation to most of the old city.
